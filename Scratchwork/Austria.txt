<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="238" data-total-count="238">VIENNA — In rejecting a far-right candidate for president on Sunday, voters in <a href="http://topics.nytimes.com/top/news/international/countriesandterritories/austria/index.html?inline=nyt-geo" title="More news and information about Austria." class="meta-loc">Austria</a> showed the limitations of President-elect Donald J. Trump’s tailwinds on a continent where extremist politics have traditionally brought cataclysm.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="135" data-total-count="373">Call it the other Trump effect, one that may sow caution among some European voters suspicious of the advances of populist politicians.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="332" data-total-count="705">Populist forces have unsettled politics in Europe and the United States, frequently by using <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2016/12/02/world/europe/italy-fake-news.html">fake news</a> and fanning fears of globalization and migration. The British vote to leave the European Union this year was complicated by such anxieties. The <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2016/12/04/world/europe/italy-matteo-renzi-referendum.html">rejection of</a><a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2016/12/04/world/europe/italy-matteo-renzi-referendum.html"> constitutional changes</a> in Italy on Sunday hinged on a variety of issues.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="57" data-total-count="762">But the choice before Austrians was perhaps the starkest.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="237" data-total-count="999">The bitter yearlong campaign for the presidency pitted Norbert Hofer, a leader of the far-right Freedom Party, founded in the 1950s by former Nazis, against a mild-mannered 72-year-old former Green Party leader, Alexander Van der Bellen.</p>        <a class="visually-hidden skip-to-text-link" href="#story-continues-1">Continue reading the main story</a>

<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="137" data-total-count="1339">In recent days, they had seemed resigned to fearing that Mr. Trump’s victory, in particular, was tilting the outcome here against them.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="251" data-total-count="1590">“It is unbelievable,” said Wolfgang Petritsch, a veteran diplomat, a biographer of the former Austrian Chancellor Bruno Kreisky and the chairman of Austria’s Marshall Plan foundation. “Austria saves the world!” he said with a twist of irony.</p><div id="story-ad-1" class="story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent">

<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="289" data-total-count="1879" id="story-continues-3">Austria is too small a sample to know whether the populist tide is abating. Europe still faces pivotal elections next year in France and Germany, as well as the possibility of a snap election in Italy. Those races will define politics in the European Union for the remainder of the decade.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="378" data-total-count="2257">But Austria does seem to disprove the idea that Mr. Trump’s victory accelerated a broader public acceptance of populist, anti-establishment forces. In recent days, many in Austria had seemed resigned to the likelihood of a Trump bounce for the far right. Mr. Hofer himself said in an interview last month that the American election had bolstered support for his Freedom Party.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="24" data-total-count="2281">Yet it was not the case.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="230" data-total-count="2511">“People followed Trump with curiosity, shock, fear, jubilation, but I don’t think they drew any conclusions,” Johannes Hübner, a Freedom Party parliamentary deputy, said on Sunday night. “It’s like a Hollywood movie.”</p><figure id="media-100000004803403" class="media photo embedded layout-large-horizontal media-100000004803403 ratio-tall" data-media-action="modal" itemprop="associatedMedia" itemscope itemid="https://static01.nyt.com/images/2016/12/05/world/05AUSTRIA3/05AUSTRIA3-master675.jpg" itemtype="http://schema.org/ImageObject" aria-label="media" role="group">

<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="110" data-total-count="2621">Perhaps the message Austrians received, he said, was “beware of another Trump — don’t vote for Hofer.”</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="123" data-total-count="2744">Mr. Van der Bellen, who exudes a calm in public, campaigned as a sort of anti-Trump, reveling in corny, retail politicking.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="266" data-total-count="3010">In an interview in September, he noted that he had been teased for donning traditional Austrian jackets and attending the numerous village fetes and festivals that are the essence of rural life here. Especially, he said, when “it emerges that I like doing this.”</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="272" data-total-count="3282">He also suggested then that the intensity of the campaign had led to a broad politicization across Austria, where the voting age is 16. <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="308" data-total-count="3720">The country, which straddles the heart of Europe from Italy and Switzerland to Hungary and Slovakia, depends heavily on tourism and exports for its comfortable living standards. A choice to be the first country in postwar Europe to elect a far-right leader for president could have jeopardized that standing.</p><div id="story-ad-2" class="story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent">

<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="274" data-total-count="3994" id="story-continues-4">The vote was widely watched across Europe as a measure of how high fringe parties could climb in mainstream politics, turning what is normally a sleepy contest for a largely ceremonial post into a measure of the populist wave that has advanced on both sides of the Atlantic.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="155" data-total-count="4149">Anti-migrant and anti-Muslim forces have already ridden a populist path to power in Hungary and Poland and have gained strength in France and even Germany.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="295" data-total-count="4444">Mr. Van der Bellen had appealed to Austrians to vote for reason over extremes. <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="258" data-total-count="4971">Establishment politicians are likely to breathe a sigh of relief and interpret the result as a sign that fringe parties still face what many in Europe have long considered a built-in barrier to broader support because of historical associations with fascism.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="123" data-total-count="5094">One person who may thank the Austrians, with whom she has tussled over immigration, is Chancellor Angela Merkel of Germany.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="212" data-total-count="5306">Austria’s rejection of the anti-European wave comes as Ms. Merkel heads this week into a congress of her conservative party, at which she will be anointed its candidate for a fourth term in elections next year.</p><div id="story-ad-3" class="story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent">

<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="169" data-total-count="5475" id="story-continues-5">Ms. Merkel issued no immediate reaction to the Austrian vote, but her vice chancellor, the Social Democratic leader Sigmar Gabriel, called it “a victory for reason.”</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="167" data-total-count="5642">Still, Mr. Hofer made the biggest advances of any populist in Europe, racking up almost 50 percent of the vote when he lost a presidential runoff <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/2016/05/24/world/europe/austria-presidential-election.html">by just 31,000 votes</a>.</p> <figure id="europe-far-right-political-parties-listy" class="interactive promo  layout-large">

<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="209" data-total-count="5851">His party contested the slim defeat, and Austria’s highest court ordered a rerun on procedural grounds. That was then postponed to Sunday, from October, after absentee ballots were found to have faulty glue.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="68" data-total-count="5919">Despite the drawn-out process, voter interest and emotions ran high.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="295" data-total-count="6214">The presidential contest was the hardest-fought since 1986, when Austrians elected Kurt Waldheim, a former United Nations secretary general, despite revelations that he had concealed his service in Hitler’s armed forces close to the sites of Nazi atrocities in the Balkans during <a href="http://topics.nytimes.com/top/reference/timestopics/subjects/w/world_war_ii_/index.html?inline=nyt-classifier" title="More articles about Wold War II." class="meta-classifier">World War II</a>.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="208" data-total-count="6422">An array of establishment figures lined up behind Mr. Van der Bellen, but Mr. Hofer garnered some support from mainstream conservatives in the People’s Party, which declined to back the former Green leader.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="291" data-total-count="6713">Mr. Petritsch and other political veterans said the length of the campaign worked in Mr. Van der Bellen’s favor. He was the clear favorite of voters in Vienna and other cities, and apparently was able to reach into rural areas where Mr. Hofer won in May but performed less strongly Sunday.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="235" data-total-count="6948">“He built a broad coalition,” said Alexandra Föderl-Schmid, the editor in chief of the liberal daily Der Standard. Now, she said, Austrians will expect Mr. Van der Bellen to mend the rifts that appeared during the bitter campaign.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="163" data-total-count="7111">After early projections showed him clearly lagging, Mr. Hofer, 45, quickly conceded on Facebook, asking “all Austrians to stick together and to work together.”</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="117" data-total-count="7228">“Dear friends,” Mr. Hofer wrote, “you supported me so well and I am infinitely sad that it did not work out.”</p><footer class="story-footer story-content">
