<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="328" data-total-count="328">MOSCOW — Now that President-elect <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/topic/person/donald-trump?inline=nyt-per" title="More articles about Donald J. Trump." class="meta-per">Donald J. Trump</a> has <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2016/12/12/us/politics/rex-tillerson-secretary-of-state-trump.html">chosen </a><a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2016/12/12/us/politics/rex-tillerson-secretary-of-state-trump.html">Rex W. Tillerson</a>, the chief executive of <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/topic/company/exxon-mobil-corporation?inline=nyt-org" title="More information about Exxon Mobil Corporation" class="meta-org">Exxon Mobil</a>, to be the next secretary of state, the giant <a href="http://topics.nytimes.com/top/news/business/energy-environment/oil-petroleum-and-gasoline/index.html?inline=nyt-classifier" title="More articles about oil." class="meta-classifier">oil</a> company stands to make some major gains as well: It has billions of dollars in deals that can go forward only if the United States lifts sanctions against <a href="http://topics.nytimes.com/top/news/international/countriesandterritories/russiaandtheformersovietunion/index.html?inline=nyt-geo" title="More news and information about Russia and the Post-Soviet Nations." class="meta-loc">Russia</a>.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="220" data-total-count="548">As head of America’s largest oil company, Mr. Tillerson has earned <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2016/12/11/business/rex-tillerson-secretary-of-state-russia.html">a friendship award</a> from Russia and voiced skepticism about American sanctions that have halted some of Exxon Mobil’s biggest projects in the country.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="170" data-total-count="718">But Mr. Tillerson’s stake in Russia’s energy industry could create a very blurry line between his interests as an oilman and his role as America’s leading diplomat.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="189" data-total-count="907">“The chances that he will view Russia with Exxon Mobil DNA are close to 100 percent,” said Robert Weissman, the president of Public Citizen, a public interest group based in Washington.</p>        <a class="visually-hidden skip-to-text-link" href="#story-continues-1">Continue reading the main story</a>
    </div><!-- close story-body -->

    <div class="story-body story-body-2">
        <p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="290" data-total-count="1197" id="story-continues-2">A tall, strapping Texan, Mr. Tillerson guided Exxon’s entry into the sharp-elbowed oil politics of Russia after the breakup of the Soviet Union. He has praised the country for its vast potential as an oil supplier ever since, developing close ties to the Kremlin leadership along the way.</p><div id="story-ad-1" class="story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent">
    <div class="accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden">
    <p>Advertisement</p>
</div>
<a class="visually-hidden skip-to-text-link" href="#story-continues-3">Continue reading the main story</a>

<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="279" data-total-count="1476" id="story-continues-3">Exxon Mobil has various projects afoot in Russia that are allowed under American sanctions. But others have been ground to a halt by the sanctions, including a deal with the Russian state oil company to explore and pump in Siberia that could be worth tens of billions of dollars.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="79" data-total-count="1555">Russian officials have optimistically called the agreement a $500 billion deal.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="229" data-total-count="1784">As for Mr. Tillerson personally, he was scheduled to retire next year from Exxon Mobil. According to company filings this year, Mr. Tillerson owned $218 million in company stock, and his pension plan was worth nearly $70 million.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="379" data-total-count="2163">Russia was already a focus of concern after the Central Intelligence Agency <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2016/12/11/us/politics/cia-judgment-intelligence-russia-hacking-evidence.html">said the Kremlin had intervened</a> in the American presidential election to help Mr. Trump’s candidacy. Now Mr. Tillerson’s career is igniting a debate over the blending of business and politics — and whether that could tip the scales in Russia’s favor on major policy decisions like the sanctions.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="345" data-total-count="2508">Speaking on Monday in Moscow, <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2016/12/08/world/europe/carter-page-donald-trump-moscow-russia.html">Carter Page</a>, a supporter of Mr. Trump who described his former work for the Trump campaign as pulling together “new ideas” on foreign policy, said he was excited that Mr. Tillerson would probably put more of an emphasis on private sector business opportunities in relations between Russia and the United States.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="174" data-total-count="2682">“What makes me excited about new possibilities is the chance to really work on new things to improve more from a business standpoint,” Mr. Page said at a news conference.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="51" data-total-count="2733">Others were much warier of a Tillerson appointment.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="271" data-total-count="3004">“As secretary of state, he would be called upon to negotiate with world leaders like Vladimir Putin,” said Michael T. Klare, a professor at Hampshire College and the author of “The Race for What’s Left,” which delves into the rush for oil in the thawing Arctic.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="249" data-total-count="3253">“In these negotiations, one has to wonder what would influence the types of deals he is making,” Mr. Klare said. “Questions arise over whether his actions would be benefiting his company or the interests of the United States and its allies.”</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="343" data-total-count="3596">Mr. Trump has called Mr. Tillerson a “player.” At an annual meet-and-greet for corporate chieftains with President Vladimir V. Putin at the St. Petersburg economic forum, Mr. Tillerson was a regular, his silver coiffure bobbing in the crowd of former spies who have become Russian government and corporate officials and now host the event.</p><div id="story-ad-2" class="story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent">
    <div class="accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden">
    <p>Advertisement</p>
</div>
<a class="visually-hidden skip-to-text-link" href="#story-continues-4">Continue reading the main story</a>
</div>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="196" data-total-count="3792" id="story-continues-4">Along with other American chief executives, Mr. Tillerson skipped the forum in 2014 to conform to White House pressure to isolate Russia, and Exxon Mobil executives insist they obey the sanctions.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="208" data-total-count="4000">“We follow the law,” said Alan Jeffers, an Exxon Mobil spokesman. “If a law says that a U.S. corporation is not allowed to participate in activities in a particular jurisdiction, that’s what we do.”</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="174" data-total-count="4174">The sanctions are intended to put economic pressure on Russia for its lethal intervention in eastern Ukraine, with the aim of forcing the Russian leadership to change course.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="73" data-total-count="4247">Still, Mr. Tillerson has made his criticism of the American policy clear.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="378" data-total-count="4625">At Exxon’s 2014 annual meeting, Mr. Tillerson said: “We do not support sanctions, generally, because we don’t find them to be effective unless they are very well implemented comprehensibly, and that’s a very hard thing to do. So we always encourage the people who are making those decisions to consider the very broad collateral damage of who are they really harming.”</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="161" data-total-count="4786">Then, during a question-and-answer period at a Houston conference in early 2015, Mr. Tillerson noted his company looked forward to the sanctions’ being lifted.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="148" data-total-count="4934">“We’ll await a time in which the sanctions environment changes or the sanctions requirements change,” he said of blocked Exxon Mobil projects.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="278" data-total-count="5212">Mr. Tillerson’s approach in Russia tracks what Robert Amsterdam, a lawyer for the Russian tycoon Mikhail B. Khodorkovsky, who had been jailed, described as “the geopolitics of signaling” to the Kremlin, a skill of survival and success for Western oil executives in Russia.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="260" data-total-count="5472">“Exxon has been willing to engage in practices that make it a first-round contender for new Russian assets,” Mr. Amsterdam said. “The way you do that is coming as close to the line as humanely possible to support the Russians” without breaking the law.</p><div id="story-ad-3" class="story-ad ad ad-placeholder nocontent robots-nocontent">
    <div class="accessibility-ad-header visually-hidden">
    <p>Advertisement</p>
</div>
<a class="visually-hidden skip-to-text-link" href="#story-continues-5">Continue reading the main story</a>
</div>
<p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="417" data-total-count="5889" id="story-continues-5">Western sanctions were first enacted on Russia in March 2014 in response to the Russian annexation of Crimea. Then the United States and its allies, including the Netherlands, implicated Russia in the shooting down of <a href="http://topics.nytimes.com/top/reference/timestopics/subjects/a/airplane_accidents_and_incidents/malaysia_airlines_flight_17/index.html?inline=nyt-classifier" title="More coverage of Malaysia Airlines Flight 17." class="meta-classifier">Malaysia Airlines Flight 17</a> over eastern Ukraine that July. All on board were killed, including 193 Dutch people heading to Asia for vacations and work, flying for a just few moments over a war zone.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="246" data-total-count="6135">That prompted tighter sanctions. A month later, Russian tanks entered eastern Ukraine, turning the tide against the forces of the American-backed Ukrainian central government. Today, about 300 American soldiers rotate through Ukraine as trainers.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="303" data-total-count="6438">After the Russian incursion in 2014, the United States prohibited the transfer of advanced offshore and shale oil technology to Russia. The American government announced on Sept. 12 that year that Exxon was to halt all <a href="http://topics.nytimes.com/top/reference/timestopics/subjects/o/offshore_drilling_and_exploration/index.html?inline=nyt-classifier" title="More articles about offshore drilling and exploration." class="meta-classifier">offshore drilling</a> assistance to Rosneft, the Russian state oil company, by Sept. 26.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="175" data-total-count="6613">But Exxon Mobil’s high-tech rig was already drilling in the Kara Sea, in an unfinished $700 million project that had yet to find oil. It would be worthless if not completed.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="296" data-total-count="6909">Russian executives then told Exxon Mobil that Russia’s security services would fly in a Russian crew — in essence seize the rig — if Exxon Mobil complied with the American law and left without completing the well, according to an oil company executive who had visited the rig in the Arctic.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="279" data-total-count="7188">Exxon relayed the threat to the American government, and the Treasury Department capitulated, granting an extension that stretched the window to work until Oct. 10. In a statement in 2014, the Russian state oil company denied conveying such a threat to Mr. Tillerson’s company.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="276" data-total-count="7464">With the extension in hand, Exxon Mobil discovered a major field with about 750 million barrels of new oil for Russia a few weeks later. Igor I. Sechin, the chief executive of the Russian state oil company, called the newly discovered oil field Pobeda — Russian for victory.</p><p class="story-body-text story-content" data-para-count="107" data-total-count="7571">It is one of the Arctic developments that Exxon Mobil has rights to work on should the sanctions be lifted.</p><footer class="story-footer story-content">
